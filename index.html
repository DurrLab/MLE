Multi-contrast Laser Endoscopy (MLE) for In Vivo Gastrointestinal Imaging

Taylor L. Bobrow, Mayank Golhar, Suchapa Arayakarnkul, Anthony A. Song, Saowanee Ngamruengphong, Nicholas J. Durr

Johns Hopkins University

   Code

Overview

Changes in endogenous chromophore concentrations, vasculature structure, and mucosal surface shape are important indicators of the progression from healthy gastrointestinal tissue to neoplasia. Imaging with engineered illumination has been used to measure these properties in ex vivo tissue, but in vivo research has been limited due to the incompatibility of these methods with flexible endoscopes and the regulatory and safety barriers associated with human testing. To overcome these challenges, we have developed a Multi-contrast Laser Endoscopy (MLE) system capable of imaging with spectral, coherent, and directional illumination in the clinical setting. We demonstrate three applications of MLE: measuring tissue oxygen saturation using multispectral diffuse reflectance, quantifying blood flow using laser speckle contrast imaging, and enhancing mucosal surface shape using photometric stereo. Following extensive benchtop validation, MLE is applied in vivo during screening colonoscopies. With multiple types of imaging contrast and the ability to seamlessly integrate into the clinical environment, MLE shows promise as an exciting new investigative tool for gastrointestinal imaging.

Retrofitted Clinical Colonoscope

To enable high-throughput experimental imaging of gastrointestinal tissues in the clinical setting, we engineered a retrofitted clinical colonoscope that accepts illumination light from both a clinical xenon source and a research illumination source.  Our system leverages the optimized optics and the CCD sensor within the endoscope to acquire HD, video rate, wide field-of-view, large depth-of-field image data. Moreover, the retrofitted colonoscope design enables near instantaneous toggling between clinical and MLE illumination modes, minimizing disruption to the clinical workflow.

Multi-contrast Laser Illumination Source

We constructed a multi-contrast illumination source from 15 laser diodes with 8 narrowband wavelengths (406-657 nm) and varying coherence lengths for acquiring speckle and speckle-free images. The source includes a custom light modulation controller that synchronizes the laser diodes with the frame capture rate of the colonoscope's CCD sensor. We also developed a custom software platform for acquiring, displaying, and storing raw uncompressed frames from the clinical endoscopy system in real time. Multiple contrast enhancement channels can be acquired with the system, including spectral, flow, and topographic measurements in addition to clinical white light illumination and narrowband imaging.

System Features

Spectral Illumination

Using spectral scanning, the system can image eight narrowband wavelengths at a rate of 7.5 frames-per-second, enabling the measurement of endogenous chromophore concentrations.

Coherent Illumination

By combining high- and low-coherence laser sources, the system can acquire speckle and speckle-free images for laser speckle contrast imaging and conventional white light imaging.

Directional Illumination

Single point sources at the tip of the scope can be toggled on for capturing directionally illuminated images, which can be processed to estimate surface topography using photometric stereo.

Autoexposure

Histogram values from the acquired frames are continuously analyzed and used to modulate the illumination output power for minimizing under- and over-exposed pixels.

Clinical Display

Frames are deinterlaced and blended in real-time to show clinicians a conventional endoscopic view while research images are acquired in the background.

Clinical Compatiability

The system retains full clinical imaging and therapeutic functionality, including compatibility with endoscope cleaning systems.

Demonstration in Human Colonoscopy

To demonstrate MLE's ability to investigate contrast enhancement in the clinical setting, we imaged adenomas in patients undergoing colonoscopy. This resulted in a dataset of 31 adenomas from 20 subjects with images acquired with both clinical and MLE illumination modes.
